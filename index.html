About Me

My research interest lies in the intersection of machine learning, medicine, and neuroscience. Body movement has fascinated me since I started dancing. Learning about the brain's motor control system has led to a general curiosity about the nervous system, its function, and pathological aberrations. The underlying cause of many neurological and psychiatric diseases is still unknown making, targeted treatment difficult. To tackle this challenge, data-driven approaches are taken to revolutionize the diagnosis and treatment of such diseases. Advanced methodology is required to make sense of the abundance of multi-modal data needed to investigate such complex diseases. My goal is to contribute to this advancement through research in computational neuroscience/medicine.

Why computational neuroscience/medicine?

Currently, diagnosis and treatment selection, especially in psychiatry, rely on self-report and subjective decisions made by physicians. Augmenting their decision process by means of machine learning could largely fasten and objectify this process. Visualizing medical data in a human comprehensible way or providing diagnoses and related uncertainties are possible augmentations. An automatic, data-driven assessment of a patient’s health status can considerably alleviate the workload of physicians, enable early disease detection, and extract meaningful insights from the abundance of the medical data available.

Research Projects

Master's Thesis at Neuronal Intelligence lab


I am currently writing my master's thesis at the Neuronal Intelligence lab under the supervision of Fabian Sinz. My main thesis objective is to model the trajectories of cognitive and motor aging in healthy elderly. By understanding the influence of risk factors from various domains (genetics, lifestyle, neuropathology, metabolism) on the trajectories, an earlier diagnosis and a targeted treatment of pathological aging could be achieved. In neurodegenerative diseases, the neurophysiological changes precede the clinical symptoms by several years, thus an early diagnosis and treatment is of high importance. Longitudinal data of healthy elderly with and without prodromal markers including genetic, biological, lifestyle, and behavioural data will be analyzed using baseline models, like generalized linear mixed effect models, and advanced Bayesian modeling.

Internship at Neuroscience of Motivation, Action, and Desire Laboratory


During my laboratory internship at NeuroMADLAB, I analyzed the reliability of functional connectivity biomarkers for Major Depressive Disorder in a multi-site dataset. Inconsistent findings of fMRI studies in the search of FC biomarkers highlight the need for large-scale, multi-site analyses which reveal a consistent picture of the disease pathology by investigating sources of site heterogeneity as the scanner type or the scanning protocol. In this mega-analysis, the functional connectivity aberrations within and between large-scale brain networks in MDD are explored using group-level analysis and supervised learning. By using cross-site validation and providing a similarity measure of the group differences between sites, the problems with small sample size studies are highlighted. Working with fMRI data and a dataset from multiple sources, I put my theoretical knowledge to practice, learned to handle messy data, and got insights into research practices.

Bachelor's Thesis


I investigated whether EEG data acquired with the Traumschreiber, a portable high-tech sleep mask, is appropriate for traditional and single-trial analysis. Within this project I designed, programmed, conducted, and analyzed an EEG study. The main result is that established standards in EEG research, like the reference placement, should be revisited for BCI applications. Breaking with those standards can in fact result in new solutions for BCI challenges, as I showed by proposing a new method to increase the sample size per participant without increasing the number of trials.
